# Presentation Architect

Build presentations that move people to action by weaving together three proven
frameworks into one coherent workflow.

## The Three Pillars

This skill synthesizes three complementary approaches. Each solves a different
problem — together they cover the full spectrum from purpose to delivery.

**Simon Sinek (Start With Why)** answers: *Why should anyone care?*
People don't buy what you do, they buy why you do it. The limbic brain — where
trust, loyalty, and decisions live — responds to purpose and belief before the
rational neocortex engages with facts. Lead with emotion, support with logic.
The Golden Circle: Why (purpose) -> How (process) -> What (result).

**Nancy Duarte (Resonate)** answers: *How do I structure the emotional journey?*
The audience is the hero, not you. You're the mentor (Yoda), they're the hero
(Luke). Structure your talk as a "sparkline" — alternating between "what is"
(current painful reality) and "what could be" (the transformed future). This
contrast creates tension that pulls the audience forward. Every great talk needs
a S.T.A.R. moment (Something They'll Always Remember) and ends with "new bliss"
— the world after they act.

**Patrick Winston (MIT)** answers: *How do I make it stick and not commit crimes?*
Open with an empowerment promise — what the audience will be able to DO by the
end. Make ideas unforgettable with Winston's Star: Symbol, Slogan, Surprise,
Salient idea, Story. Ruthlessly audit slides against the 10 slide crimes.
Close with contributions, not "thank you."

For the detailed breakdown of each framework (including Winston's 10 slide
crimes, the full Star methodology, and Duarte's sparkline mechanics), read
`references/frameworks.md`.

## How the Frameworks Layer Together

| Layer | Source | What It Does |
|-------|--------|--------------|
| Purpose | Sinek's Why | Why this talk exists, why the audience should care |
| Narrative arc | Duarte's Sparkline | Emotional structure — tension between what is / what could be |
| Stickiness | Winston's Star | Hooks that make the idea impossible to forget |
| Opening | Winston's Promise | Specific outcome the audience walks away with |
| Climax | Duarte's S.T.A.R. | The single dramatic moment that brands the talk in memory |
| Slides | Winston's Crimes | Ruthless audit of what kills presentations |
| Close | All three combined | Contributions + New Bliss + Why callback |

## Interactive Workflow

This is a conversation, not a template dump. Work through each phase in order.
Ask questions, wait for answers, and build each phase on the user's own words.
Don't skip ahead or fill in blanks with generic content — specificity is what
makes a presentation powerful.

Adapt the depth of each phase to the user's situation. A quick team update
doesn't need the same treatment as a keynote. Read the room.

---

### Phase 1: Discovery — Find the Why and the Big Idea

Start every engagement here. Ask these questions (adapt phrasing to feel natural,
but cover all five):

1. **What's the presentation about?** — topic, context, occasion, time limit
2. **Who's in the audience?** — their role, expertise, what they care about, what
   keeps them up at night
3. **What do you want them to DO after?** — a specific action, not "understand"
   or "be aware of"
4. **Why does this matter — to you personally, and to them?** — the belief or
   conviction driving this (Sinek's Why)
5. **What's at stake if they don't act?** — urgency, cost of inaction, what they
   lose

From their answers, synthesize:

**The Why Statement** (Sinek):
> "I believe [belief] because [evidence/conviction], and this talk exists to
> [action it enables]."

**The Big Idea** (Duarte):
> "[Specific audience] can [specific transformation] by [specific approach], and
> the cost of not doing this is [specific consequence]."

Present both back to the user. Refine until they feel right before proceeding.
These two statements are the foundation — everything else builds on them.

---

### Phase 2: Empowerment Promise + Sparkline Arc

**Craft the Empowerment Promise** (Winston):

Write a specific, outcome-driven opening promise. The format:

> "By the end of this talk, you will be able to [specific capability]."

The promise must be:
- Specific enough to be tested (could the audience verify they got it?)
- Valuable enough to earn their full attention
- Connected to the Why from Phase 1

Bad: "You'll learn about our new strategy."
Good: "You'll walk out of here able to explain to your team exactly why we're
pivoting — and what to do Monday morning."

**Design the Sparkline Arc** (Duarte):

Ask the user: *"What's the current painful reality your audience is living with?
And what does the transformed future look like if they act on your message?"*

Then sketch 3-4 alternating beats:

```
WHAT IS  (current pain)        ->  WHAT COULD BE (glimpse of possibility)
WHAT IS  (why it's hard)       ->  WHAT COULD BE (the method/solution)
WHAT IS  (cost of inaction)    ->  WHAT COULD BE (proof it works)
                                   NEW BLISS     (the transformed world)
```

**Map the Golden Circle** (Sinek) onto the arc:
- **Why** anchors the opening and close
- **How** fills the middle — the method, process, or approach
- **What** provides concrete proof points (data, examples, demos)

### Phase 3: Winston's Star — Make It Unforgettable

Work through each Star element one at a time. For each, propose 2-3 options
and let the user choose or riff. The user's instinct matters here — they know
their audience.

1. **Symbol** — A visual or physical object that represents the core idea
   instantly. Must be concrete and specific, not abstract.
   *Test: Can someone sketch it in 5 seconds?*

2. **Slogan** — A short phrase that becomes the handle people use to repeat the
   idea. Must be repeatable in a meeting without explanation.
   *Test: Would someone text this to a colleague?*

3. **Surprise** — The counterintuitive truth that makes people stop and
   reconsider. Must genuinely challenge an assumption the audience holds.
   *Test: Would someone say "wait, really?" when they hear it?*

4. **Salient Idea** — THE one idea that rises above everything else. Exactly
   one. If you can't pick one, the talk isn't focused enough.
   *Test: If the audience remembers only one thing, is this it?*

5. **Story** — The narrative that makes the salient idea human: how it was
   discovered, why it matters, the journey. Personal enough to be specific,
   universal enough to resonate.
   *Test: Does it have a character, tension, and a turning point?*

---

### Phase 4: The S.T.A.R. Moment — The Unforgettable Peak

Design the single most memorable moment — the thing people describe when they
tell someone else about the talk.

Four types of S.T.A.R. moments (Duarte), enhanced with Winston's prop principles:

- **Dramatic demonstration** — a live demo, physical prop, or real-time reveal.
  The prop must be physical and demonstrable (Winston) — not a slide.
- **Shocking statistic** — a number so surprising it reframes everything.
  Present it visually for maximum impact.
- **Emotional story** — a human moment that creates genuine feeling. Needs real
  tension before the resolution (Winston).
- **Visual reveal** — an image or visual that makes the abstract viscerally
  concrete.

Ask: *"What's the one moment you want people to talk about at dinner tonight?"*

Design the moment, including:
- What it is and how to execute it
- Where it falls in the sparkline (usually at the peak of tension)
- The setup — how to build tension before the reveal
- The landing — how to connect it back to the salient idea

---

### Phase 5: Slide Design

**If the user has existing slides**: audit against Winston's 10 Slide Crimes
(see `references/frameworks.md` for the full list with specific fixes for each).
Flag every crime with a concrete fix.

**If starting from scratch**: build a slide-by-slide outline from the sparkline
arc. For each slide, note:
- Whether it's a "what is" or "what could be" beat
- The single idea or image it carries
- Whether it's a visual slide, a data slide, or a "blank" storytelling moment

Core slide principles (all three frameworks):
- **One idea per slide** — if it needs a paragraph, it needs a conversation
- **Minimum 40pt font** — no exceptions (Winston)
- **Glance test** — understood in 3 seconds (Duarte)
- **Slides are condiments, not the meal** — you are the presentation (Winston)
- **Visual contrast mirrors emotional contrast** — "what is" slides feel
  different from "what could be" slides (Duarte)
- **Final slide is contributions** — never "Thank you" or "Questions?" (Winston).
  It stays up during Q&A.

---

### Phase 6: Opening Script + Close

**Opening (first 60 seconds)**:

Write a draft script that:
1. Opens with "what is" tension (Duarte) — drop the audience into the current
   reality. No preamble, no "good morning," no jokes, no "thank you for having
   me" (Winston).
2. Delivers the empowerment promise (Winston) — what they'll walk away able to do.
3. Connects to the Why (Sinek) — make them feel it, not just understand it.

The first 60 seconds must earn the next 60 minutes. Every word must serve the
promise.

**Close**:

Write a draft script that:
1. Returns to the Why (Sinek) — mirror the opening belief
2. Paints the New Bliss (Duarte) — the transformed future if they act
3. Ends on Contributions (Winston) — what was specifically delivered, proving
   the empowerment promise was kept

The open and close are bookends: promise made, promise kept.

## Adapting by Presentation Type

Adjust emphasis based on context — not every phase needs equal depth:

| Type | Emphasize | De-emphasize |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| Keynote / Conference | Star, S.T.A.R., Sparkline | Slide audit (they expect polish) |
| Sales / Investor pitch | Why, Promise, Proof of Work | Props (keep it tight) |
| Board / Executive | Big Idea, Contributions, Slides | Story length (respect their time) |
| Educational / Workshop | Props, Story, Promise | Slogan (they need skills, not slogans) |
| Team update / All-hands | Why, New Bliss | Full Star (keep it short and purposeful) |

## Guiding Principles

These hold across every phase:

- **The audience is the hero** — every choice serves them, not you
- **One salient idea** — a talk that tries to say three things says nothing
- **Emotion first, logic second** — the limbic brain decides, the neocortex justifies
- **Tension creates attention** — the gap between what is and what could be is your engine
- **Specificity is persuasion** — vague is forgettable, concrete is compelling
- **Promise made, promise kept** — open and close are bookends
