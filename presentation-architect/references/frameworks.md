## Winston's 10 Slide Crimes

Every crime gets a specific fix — flagging without a fix is useless.

| # | Crime | Why It Kills | Specific Fix |
|---|-------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | Too many slides | Audience loses the thread | Cut ruthlessly. If a slide doesn't advance the empowerment promise, delete it. Aim for 1 slide per 2-3 minutes of talk time as a ceiling. |
| 2 | Too many words per slide | Audience reads ahead, stops listening | One idea per slide. Max 15 words. If you need more, you need a conversation, not a slide. |
| 3 | Font size under 40pt | Back rows can't read, everyone squints | Minimum 40pt, no exceptions. If it doesn't fit at 40pt, the slide has too much content — split it. |
| 4 | Reading slides aloud | Audience feels patronized, disengages | Slides are visual reinforcement, not your script. Say something different from what's on screen — add context, story, or emphasis. |
| 5 | Laser pointer usage | Signals the slide is too complex to parse | If you need to point at something, the slide needs simplifying. Use animation builds, highlights, or callout slides instead. |
| 6 | Speaker standing far from slides | Audience has to choose where to look | Stay near the screen. Your physical presence next to the visual creates a unified focal point. |
| 7 | No white space or air | Slides feel claustrophobic, brain has no room | White space is breathing room for the audience's brain. Embrace margins. Remove anything that isn't earning its place. |
| 8 | Background clutter and logos | Visual noise competes with content | Strip everything that isn't content. Corporate logos and templates go on the first slide and last slide only — nowhere else. |
| 9 | Collaborators list as final slide | Weak, forgettable, signals "I'm done" | Final slide is your contributions — what you delivered, what the audience gained. |
| 10 | "Thank you" or "Questions?" as final slide | Weakest possible ending — gives audience permission to forget | End on contributions. The contributions slide stays up during Q&A. If someone walks in late and sees only one slide, it should be this one. |

## Winston's Star

The five elements that make any idea impossible to forget. Each must be crafted
deliberately — they don't emerge by accident.

### Symbol
A visual or physical object that represents the core idea instantly.

- Must be concrete and specific — not abstract
- Should be sketchable in 5 seconds
- Works because the visual cortex processes images 60,000x faster than text
- Example: The "iceberg" for hidden complexity. A "bridge" for connecting teams.
  Steve Jobs pulling a MacBook Air from a manila envelope.

### Slogan
A short phrase that becomes the handle people use to remember and repeat the idea.

- Must be repeatable in a meeting without needing explanation
- Ideally under 8 words
- Should capture the salient idea, not just sound catchy
- Test: would someone text this to a colleague after the talk?
- Example: "Move fast and break things." "What got you here won't get you there."

### Surprise
The counterintuitive truth that makes people stop and think.

- Must genuinely challenge an assumption the audience holds
- Not just "interesting" — it should provoke a "wait, really?" reaction
- Often takes the form: "You think X, but actually Y"
- The surprise creates a gap in understanding that the rest of the talk fills
- Example: "The most productive teams aren't the ones with the best people —
  they're the ones with the most psychological safety."

### Salient Idea
THE one idea that sticks out above everything else.

- Must be exactly one — never two or three
- If you can't pick one, the talk isn't focused enough yet
- Every other element of the Star should point back to this
- The salient idea is what you want the audience to say when someone asks
  "what was that talk about?"

### Story
The narrative that makes the salient idea feel human and real.

- How the idea was discovered, why it matters, the journey that led here
- Must have a character, tension, and a turning point
- Personal enough to be specific, universal enough to resonate
- The story isn't decoration — it's the vehicle that carries the idea into
  long-term memory

## Winston's Empowerment Promise

The empowerment promise is the contract between speaker and audience. It tells
them exactly what they'll be able to DO by the end — not what they'll "learn
about" or "understand."

**Formula**: "By the end of this [talk/session/presentation], you will be able
to [specific, testable capability]."

**Rules**:
- Never open with a joke — the audience isn't ready, and it's a gamble
- Never open with "thank you for having me" — it's weak and forgettable
- The promise must be specific — not "you'll learn about X" but "you'll be
  able to do Y"
- The first 60 seconds must earn the next 60 minutes
- Cut everything from the opening that doesn't serve the promise

**What makes a good promise**:
- Falsifiable: the audience could test whether they got it
- Valuable: worth their time to stay and listen
- Achievable: you can actually deliver on it in the time you have

## Winston's Job Talk Framework

Originally designed for academic job talks, this structure works for any
presentation that needs to convince, convert, or close.

**Three pillars**: Vision, Proof of Work, Contributions

1. **Vision** — The problem you see that others don't, and your approach to it.
   Must be established within the first 5 minutes — never later.

2. **Proof of Work** — The specific steps you've taken that prove you've done
   something real. Must be concrete steps, not vague accomplishments.
   "We ran 47 experiments across 3 markets" beats "we did extensive testing."

3. **Contributions** — What you specifically delivered. The final slide. Stays
   up during Q&A. Mirrors the opening promise.

**Rules**:
- Vision within 5 minutes — never later
- Opening and close must mirror each other — promise made, promise kept
- Every minute must advance either vision or proof — nothing else
- Contributions slide never replaced with "thank you"

## Winston's Props & Storytelling

Physical demonstrations make abstract ideas tangible.

**Prop principles**:
- Must be physical and demonstrable — not a slide or diagram
- Story around it needs genuine tension before the resolution
- Guide attention explicitly — tell the audience where to look and what to notice
- Design it so even failure teaches something
- If no physical prop exists, design the closest verbal equivalent

**Story structure**:
1. Identify the single most confusing aspect of the idea
2. Design a prop or demonstration that makes the confusion disappear
3. Build a story around the prop: tension -> demonstration -> resolution
4. Write a verbal script that guides from confusion to clarity

## Duarte's Sparkline

The sparkline is the emotional structure of a presentation — the shape of the
audience's journey.

**Core mechanic**: Alternate between two states:
- **What Is** — the current reality, the status quo, the pain point. This is
  where the audience lives right now.
- **What Could Be** — the future possibility, the transformed state. This is
  where you're taking them.

The contrast between these two states creates tension. Tension creates attention.
Each "what could be" moment should feel like a relief from the "what is" tension,
pulling the audience toward your vision.

**Full sparkline shape**:
```
What Is    →  What Could Be
  ↓              ↓
What Is    →  What Could Be
  ↓              ↓
What Is    →  What Could Be
                 ↓
              NEW BLISS (the call to action + transformed future)
```

**The "what is" beats** should feel progressively more urgent:
1. First: the surface-level problem everyone recognizes
2. Second: the deeper, less obvious cause
3. Third: the cost of inaction — what happens if nothing changes

**The "what could be" beats** should feel progressively more concrete:
1. First: a glimpse of possibility
2. Second: the method or approach that gets there
3. Third: proof it works (data, story, example)

**New Bliss**: The final state. Paint the picture of the world after the
audience acts. This is aspirational but grounded — not fantasy, but an
achievable future they now believe in because of your talk.

## Duarte's S.T.A.R. Moment Types

**S.T.A.R. = Something They'll Always Remember**

Every presentation should have one moment so impactful that the audience
remembers it years later. This is the peak of your sparkline.

### Type 1: Memorable Dramatization
A live performance or enactment that makes the idea visceral.
- Bill Gates releasing mosquitoes during a malaria talk
- A speaker smashing a product to show its weakness

### Type 2: Repeatable Sound Bite
A phrase so perfectly crafted it spreads on its own.
- "I have a dream..." (MLK)
- "One more thing..." (Jobs)

### Type 3: Evocative Visual
An image or visual so powerful it needs no words.
- The pale blue dot (Sagan)
- Before/after comparison photos

### Type 4: Shocking Statistic
A number that reframes everything the audience thought they knew.
- Must be presented with contrast to land ("That's more than [thing they understand]")
- Visual representation amplifies impact

### Type 5: Emotional Story
A human story that creates genuine feeling — joy, outrage, hope, surprise.
- Must have a specific character (not "people" but "Maria, a nurse in Detroit")
- Needs real tension and stakes
- Resolution connects back to the salient idea

**Placement**: The S.T.A.R. moment usually falls at the peak of the sparkline
tension — the moment of maximum contrast between what is and what could be.

## Duarte's Big Idea Formula

The Big Idea is one sentence that captures your entire presentation. It has
two components:

1. **Your unique point of view** — what you believe that not everyone agrees with
2. **What's at stake** — why the audience should care right now

**Formula**: "[Audience] can [transformation] by [approach], and if they don't,
[consequence]."

**Test**: If you can only say one sentence before being cut off, is this the
sentence that would make the audience want to hear more?

The Big Idea is not a topic ("I'm talking about customer retention"). It's a
point of view with stakes ("We're losing 30% of customers in the first 90 days,
and the fix isn't better onboarding — it's killing three features that actively
drive them away").

## Sinek's Golden Circle — Applied

The Golden Circle is a communication framework based on how the brain processes
trust and decision-making.

**The three layers** (communicate from inside out):
- **Why** (core): Your purpose, cause, or belief. Why you get out of bed.
  Why anyone should care. This speaks to the limbic brain — the part that
  controls feelings, trust, loyalty, and all decision-making.
- **How** (middle): Your process, method, or differentiating approach. The
  principles that guide your work.
- **What** (outer): Your results, products, features, facts. This speaks to
  the neocortex — rational, analytical thought.

**Applied to presentations**:
- **Open with Why**: Don't start with what you did or how you did it. Start
  with why it matters. The audience decides whether to trust you in the first
  minutes — and trust is a limbic (emotional) decision.
- **Middle is How**: Once they believe in the why, walk them through the
  approach. This is where the sparkline's "what could be" beats align.
- **Support with What**: Facts, data, and specifics come after the emotional
  buy-in. They justify the decision the limbic brain has already made.

**Key insight**: Most presentations go outside-in (What -> How -> Why). Great
presentations go inside-out (Why -> How -> What). The audience doesn't need
to be convinced of the facts — they need to feel the purpose first. The facts
then feel inevitable.

**Inspiration vs. Manipulation**: Sinek distinguishes between two ways to
influence behavior. Manipulation (price, fear, pressure, aspiration) works
short-term but doesn't build loyalty. Inspiration (shared belief, purpose,
belonging) works long-term and creates advocates. Build presentations that
inspire, not manipulate.
